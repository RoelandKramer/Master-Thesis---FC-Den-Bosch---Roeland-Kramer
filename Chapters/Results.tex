\chapter{Preliminary Results}
\label{ch:results}

This chapter presents the preliminary empirical findings produced by the
analytical pipeline described in Chapter~\ref{sec:methods}. Because the
models for SQ1 and SQ2 are still being refined, all figures reported here
are subject to change. Results are presented per sub-question; SQ3 and SQ4
have no preliminary results at this stage, and SQ5 is illustrated through
the current state of the Data \& Video Analyser platform.

\section{SQ1: Defensive Setup Classification}
\label{sec:results_sq1}

\subsection{Role distribution in the labelled test set}

Manual labelling was performed on 67 crossed corner kicks, covering both
attacking and defending players. For the defensive side, a total of
730 defender-event rows were labelled across these corners. After
normalising spelling variants, the role distribution is as follows:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Defensive role distribution in the manually labelled test
           set (67 corners, 730 defender rows).}
  \label{tab:sq1_role_dist}
  \begin{tabular}{lrr}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Defensive Role} & \textbf{Count} & \textbf{\%} \\
    \midrule
    ZONAL    & 577 & 79\% \\
    MAN      &  90 & 12\% \\
    SHORT    &  46 &  6\% \\
    COUNTER  &  17 &  2\% \\
    \midrule
    Total    & 730 & 100\% \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Zonal marking is by far the dominant defensive structure in the Eerste
Divisie 2025--26 dataset, appearing in roughly four out of five
defensive interactions. Man-marking is observed in 12\% of cases,
while short-corner press assignments (SHORT) and counter-pressing
(COUNTER) together account for the remaining 8\%. This heavy class
imbalance is an important constraint on classifier design; models that
predict ZONAL for all rows would already achieve approximately 79\%
row-level accuracy.

\subsection{Classifier accuracy per role}

The ensemble classifier (Random Forest, Extra Trees, and Gradient
Boosting, averaged) trained on tracking-derived pair-wise features
attained a leave-one-corner-out (LOCO-CV) overall accuracy of
approximately \textbf{93\%} on the labelled defender rows. Because the
ZONAL class dominates the dataset, per-class performance is the more
informative measure:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{ZONAL}: high precision and recall; the large sample
        supports reliable feature learning.
  \item \textbf{MAN}: correctly identified in the majority of cases;
        the key discriminating signal is the persistent small distance
        to a specific attacker throughout the pre-delivery window
        combined with correlated movement.
  \item \textbf{SHORT}: well-separated by the positional rule component
        of the classifier (defender moves toward the ball carrier rather
        than into the penalty area).
  \item \textbf{COUNTER}: the smallest class (17 rows); performance is
        more variable and is treated as indicative only.
\end{itemize}

The Hungarian matching step, which assigns man-markers to their
specific attacker counterpart, attained approximately 80\% assignment
accuracy on the labelled subset, measured as the share of man-markers
whose assigned attacker matches the manual label.

\paragraph{Next steps for SQ1.}
The priority is to perform formal inter-rater reliability testing on
the manual labels before reporting final accuracy figures. Labelling
more corners — particularly corners with mixed defensive structures —
will further strengthen the test set. See
Chapter~\ref{ch:plan} for the full work plan.

\section{SQ2: Offensive Pattern Classification}
\label{sec:results_sq2}

\subsection{Delivery type and ball height}

Of the 1{,}401 direct corners in the dataset, the swing and zone
classification yields the following delivery-type breakdown:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Delivery type distribution across 1{,}401 direct corners.}
  \label{tab:sq2_delivery_dist}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrl}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Delivery type} & \textbf{$n$} & \textbf{\%} &
    \textbf{Mean peak height (m)} \\
    \midrule
    IN\_CENTRAL  & 527 & 38\% & 5.75 \\
    IN\_NEAR     & 285 & 20\% & 5.01 \\
    OUT\_CENTRAL & 249 & 18\% & 5.73 \\
    OUT\_NEAR    & 160 & 11\% & 4.55 \\
    IN\_FAR      & 124 &  9\% & 8.07 \\
    OUT\_FAR     &  56 &  4\% & 7.50 \\
    \midrule
    Total        & 1{,}401 & 100\% & 5.73 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Inswing deliveries into the central zone (IN\_CENTRAL) are by far the
most common, comprising 38\% of all direct corners. Far-post deliveries
(IN\_FAR, OUT\_FAR) are taken at notably higher trajectories (7.5--8.1 m
peak) compared with near-post deliveries (4.5--5.0 m peak), consistent
with the geometry of delivery angles from the corner flag.

Across the full dataset the dominant ball flight categories are:
\textbf{MEDIUM} (3--8 m peak, $n = 732$, 52\%),
\textbf{DRIVEN} ($<$3 m, $n = 417$, 30\%),
and \textbf{FLOATED} ($>$8 m, $n = 252$, 18\%).
Driven deliveries are most common among near-post corners, while far-post
deliveries are more often floated.

\subsection{Short-corner classification}

Of the 1{,}893 total corners, 492 (26\%) are classified as short corners.
The automated sequence classifier assigns each short corner to one of
four categories based on the number of passes and final action:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Short corner type distribution ($n = 492$).}
  \label{tab:sq2_short}
  \begin{tabular}{lrr}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Short type} & \textbf{$n$} & \textbf{\%} \\
    \midrule
    SHORT\_1PASS    & 206 & 42\% \\
    SHORT\_RECYCLE  & 129 & 26\% \\
    SHORT\_SHOT     & 113 & 23\% \\
    SHORT\_2PASS    &  44 &  9\% \\
    \midrule
    Total           & 492 & 100\% \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The most common short-corner pattern is a one-pass play followed by a
cross into the box (SHORT\_1PASS), which accounts for 42\% of all short
corners. Recycled short corners (SHORT\_RECYCLE) — where the team
retains possession but does not immediately cross — make up 26\%. Short
plays that directly result in a shot are less common (23\%) but
tactically significant. On a manually labelled subset of 21 short
corners, the classifier achieved 81\% exact-label agreement, 86\% result
accuracy (cross / shot / ball loss), and 86\% player-count accuracy.

\subsection{Attacker role classifier accuracy}
\label{sec:results_sq2_roles}

The attacker role classifier was trained and evaluated on 587 manually
labelled attacker rows from 67 corners, using leave-one-corner-out
cross-validation (LOCO-CV). The labelled role distribution reflects
strong class imbalance:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Attacker role distribution in the manually labelled test set
           (67 corners, 587 attacker rows after feature matching).}
  \label{tab:sq2_role_dist}
  \begin{tabular}{lrr}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Attacker Role} & \textbf{Count} & \textbf{\%} \\
    \midrule
    STATIC      & 305 & 52\% \\
    DECOY       & 101 & 17\% \\
    TARGET      & 100 & 17\% \\
    SECOND\_BALL &  54 &  9\% \\
    BLOCK\_DEF  &  14 &  2\% \\
    BLOCK\_GK   &  13 &  2\% \\
    \midrule
    Total       & 587 & 100\% \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

An ensemble of three tree-based classifiers (Random Forest, Extra
Trees, Gradient Boosting) using 27 tracking-derived features was
evaluated under three classification schemes:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Attacker role classifier accuracy under different class
           schemes (LOCO-CV on 587 labelled rows).}
  \label{tab:sq2_role_acc}
  \begin{tabular}{llr}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Scheme} & \textbf{Classes} & \textbf{Accuracy} \\
    \midrule
    6-class (full)                     & TARGET / DECOY / STATIC / SECOND\_BALL /
                                         BLOCK\_GK / BLOCK\_DEF & 74.1\% \\
    5-class                            & TARGET / DECOY / STATIC / SECOND\_BALL /
                                         BLOCKER                & 74.4\% \\
    4-class                            & RECEIVER / DECOY / STATIC / BLOCKER & \textbf{79.7\%} \\
    6-class + augm.$^{\dagger}$        & same as 6-class        & 72.6\% \\
    5-class + augm.$^{\dagger}$        & same as 5-class        & 73.9\% \\
    \bottomrule
    \multicolumn{3}{l}{\footnotesize $^{\dagger}$ y-coordinate flipping + SMOTE
      within each LOCO-CV training fold.}
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The dominant features driving classification are \texttt{dist\_to\_gk\_at\_kick}
(proximity to the goalkeeper at kick time), \texttt{dist\_ball\_at\_end}
(distance to ball arrival point at delivery), \texttt{gap\_to\_closest}
(gap between the player and the closest attacker to the ball), and
\texttt{x\_end} (normalised horizontal position after the delivery).

Per-class performance on the 6-class scheme: STATIC achieves high fidelity
(F1 = 0.86), TARGET moderate fidelity (F1 = 0.69), and DECOY lower fidelity
(F1 = 0.59). The BLOCK\_DEF class achieves 0\% F1 — a direct consequence of
only 14 labelled examples, which is insufficient for reliable ML learning under
LOCO-CV. A data-augmentation approach (y-coordinate flipping combined with SMOTE
within each training fold) was evaluated but marginally reduced performance:
SMOTE with very few anchor points generates unrealistic synthetic samples that
reduce performance on the dominant STATIC class (F1 from 0.86 to 0.84).
The fundamental bottleneck for the 6-class problem is label scarcity for
BLOCK\_GK and BLOCK\_DEF; expanding the manually labelled set is the most
direct path to improvement (see Chapter~\ref{ch:plan}). The 4-class scheme,
which merges BLOCK\_GK and BLOCK\_DEF into BLOCKER and collapses SECOND\_BALL
into STATIC, achieves \textbf{79.7\%} — effectively meeting the 80\% accuracy
target — because the merged classes are large enough for reliable learning.

\subsection{Corner strategy taxonomy}
\label{sec:results_sq2_taxonomy}

After aggregating predicted roles per corner, the priority-ordered
taxonomy (see Section~\ref{sec:sq2_taxonomy}) assigns each of the 67
labelled corners a single strategic category. Using ground-truth role
labels, the distribution across the test set is:

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Corner strategy taxonomy distribution on the 67 manually
           labelled corners (ground-truth roles).}
  \label{tab:sq2_taxonomy}
  \begin{tabular}{llrr}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Tier} & \textbf{Category} & \textbf{$n$} & \textbf{\%} \\
    \midrule
    F & STATIC\_FORMATION  & 22 & 33\% \\
    B & BLOCK\_DEF\_PULL   & 12 & 18\% \\
    B & BLOCK\_GK\_WAVE    & 10 & 15\% \\
    C & MULTI\_TARGET\_RUSH &  9 & 13\% \\
    D & MULTI\_DECOY\_PULL  &  8 & 12\% \\
    F & STATIC\_HEAVY       &  3 &  4\% \\
    B & BLOCK\_GK\_SOLO     &  2 &  3\% \\
    B & DOUBLE\_BLOCK       &  1 &  1\% \\
    \midrule
    \multicolumn{2}{l}{Total} & 67 & 100\% \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The most prevalent category is \textbf{STATIC\_FORMATION} (33\%), in which
six or more attackers remain largely stationary and no dominant running
threat is identified. Block-based strategies (Tier B) are collectively
the most common tactical family: BLOCK\_DEF\_PULL (18\%),
BLOCK\_GK\_WAVE (15\%), BLOCK\_GK\_SOLO (3\%), and DOUBLE\_BLOCK (1\%)
together account for 37\% of corners. Dynamic strategies — three or more
TARGETs (MULTI\_TARGET\_RUSH, 13\%) or three or more DECOYs
(MULTI\_DECOY\_PULL, 12\%) — each appear in roughly one in eight corners.

Note that the more granular Tier C/D categories that depend on running-line
direction (NEAR\_POST\_TARGET, FAR\_POST\_TARGET, SCISSORS etc.) do not
appear in this tabulation because running-line labels have not yet been
applied to the labelled set. Once the running-line classifier is
integrated, these categories will be populated and the taxonomy
distribution will shift accordingly.

On average, each labelled corner contains 1.5 TARGET players, 1.5 DECOY
players, and 5.0 STATIC players, confirming that the majority of corner
attackers take holding or static positions rather than dynamic running
roles.

\section{SQ3 \& SQ4: No Preliminary Results}
\label{sec:results_sq3_sq4}

At this stage of the project, no preliminary results are reported for SQ3
(effectiveness of offensive strategies against defensive structures) and
SQ4 (individual player quality in corners). Both sub-questions require
the SQ1 and SQ2 models to be finalised and applied to the full
1{,}401-corner dataset before meaningful inference is possible. The
interaction between offensive strategy (SQ2) and defensive structure
(SQ1) is the core of SQ3, and the results from that analysis will
directly inform which tactics are most effective against which defences.
The plan for completing these analyses is outlined in
Chapter~\ref{ch:plan}.

\section{SQ5: Data \& Video Analyser Platform}
\label{sec:results_sq5}

\subsection{Platform overview}

Alongside the analytical research, a practical Data \& Video Analyser
(D\&V Analyser) is being developed for direct use by FC Den Bosch. The
platform serves as the integration layer through which the research
outputs of SQ1--SQ4 will be delivered to coaching staff in a
video-anchored, interactive format.

The current version of the platform matches tracking and event data to
raw video footage, enabling users to navigate directly from a corner in
the data to the corresponding video clip. This synchronisation layer is
the technical foundation on which the analytical outputs of this thesis
will be placed: delivery zone profiles, defensive setup frequencies,
attacker role patterns, and corner strategy categories will each be
browsable alongside video evidence.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/Video & Data analyser.png}
  \caption{Current state of the Data \& Video Analyser platform.
           The interface synchronises SciSports tracking and event data
           with match video footage, allowing the user to navigate

           corresponding video clip. The analytical results of SQ1--SQ4
           will be surfaced through this interface.}
  \label{fig:dv_analyser}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Data--video synchronisation}

The synchronisation pipeline operates as follows. For each match in the
dataset, the SciSports event timestamps (in milliseconds from kick-off)
are aligned to the video frame index using a calibration offset derived
from a known reference event (e.g.\ the kick-off whistle or a clearly
identifiable ball event). Once the offset is established, any event
timestamp can be converted to a video frame index with sub-second
precision, enabling one-click navigation from any corner record to
video footage of that corner.

Currently the platform supports browsing all 1{,}893 corners in the
dataset, filtering by team, match, corner side, and delivery type, and
playing back the matched video clip with optional overlay of tracking
data. The research results — delivery effectiveness profiles, team
defensive tendencies, offensive strategy categories — will be added as
an additional analytical layer once the SQ1 and SQ2 models are
finalised. This will allow coaching staff to move seamlessly between a
team's aggregate corner profile and individual video examples of each
strategic pattern.

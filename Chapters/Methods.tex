\chapter{Methods}

\section{Research Design}
\label{sec:research_design}

This study adopts a quantitative, data-driven research design that sequentially
builds five analytical layers, each corresponding to one sub-question (SQ).
The overarching logic is as follows: corner kicks are first characterised from both
sides of the ball (SQ1: defensive setup; SQ2: offensive pattern), then matched to
quantify effectiveness (SQ3), enriched with individual player attributes (SQ4), and
finally synthesised into a practical preparation tool (SQ5).
SQ1 \& SQ2 are tested on a manually labeled testset, and then once verificed, all analyses are applied to the entire dataset.
Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} provides an overview of the analytical pipeline.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Data Sources}
\label{sec:data_sources}

All data were provided by SciSports through FC Den Bosch. Two data types were used.

\subsubsection{Event data.}
Event data contains one record per on-ball action, including the action type
(e.g.\ \texttt{CORNER\_CROSSED}, \texttt{CORNER\_SHORT}, \texttt{HEADER},
\texttt{CLEARANCE}), the player and team involved, the ball's start and end
coordinates in metres (origin at pitch centre, $x$-axis along the length,
$y$-axis along the width), the timestamp in milliseconds (steps of 100ms), and derived metrics
such as delivery length, angle, expected goals (xG), and expected assists (xA).
Additionally, each event contains a \texttt{sequenceId} that groups all actions
within a single possession sequence, enabling reconstruction of what happened
after every corner kick. Event data is labeled by workers of SciSports, and is available within 
48 hours of every match in the KeukenKampioenDivisie.

\subsubsection{Tracking data.}
Tracking data records the $(x, y)$ position of every player and the ball at
10~Hz (one frame per 100~ms). Player records include a unique player identifier
and shirt number. Ball records include an additional $z$-coordinate (height in
metres) allowing computation of ball flight characteristics.
Tracking data are provided in positional JSON files that can be aligned to the same
millisecond clock as the event data. SciSports includes the variable \texttt{synced} in the
event data to indicate whether a given event can be reliably matched to tracking data; 
all corners in the dataset that are not synchronised have been removed from the analysis. Tracking data
is created by a computer vision pipeline run by SciSports that processes the broadcast 
video feed, and is available within 48 hours of every match in the KeukenKampioenDivisie.

\subsection{Manual Labelling}
\label{sec:manual_labelling}

A subset of 101 synced corners were randomly placed into a manual labelling queue, stratified 
by club and corner type (direct vs.\ short) to ensure representativeness. Of these, 
86 were successfully labeled before the analysis phase; the remaining 15 were not due to 
bad camera angles on the videos, which made it impossible to reliably annotate the required variables. 
The 86 labelled corners are a subset of the 1,893 corners that were synced and therefore 
kept. For each corner, the following were annotated:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Delivery type}: swing direction and target zone (see
        Section~\ref{sec:sq2_delivery});
  \item \textbf{Ball flight}: one of five trajectory categories
        (see Section~\ref{sec:sq2_flight});
  \item \textbf{Attacker run roles}: per-player tactical role for all attacking
        outfield players (see Section~\ref{sec:sq2_runs});
  \item \textbf{Defender roles}: per-player defensive role
        (see Section~\ref{sec:sq1});
  \item \textbf{Man-marking assignments}: for defenders classified as man-markers,
        the jersey number of the specific attacker being tracked.
\end{itemize}
Labels were recorded in a structured spreadsheet
(\texttt{labeling\_sheet\_v2.csv}) and used as ground truth for model training
and validation.
Inter-rater reliability is not yet formally assessed, as only one annotator was
used; this is something that will be done later in the project timeline.

\paragraph{Zone system.}
The pitch area surrounding the goal was divided according to the zone system
depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:{zone_system}}. A central column of width 18.32~m
(the goal area width) was extended perpendicularly from the goal line up to
the edge of the penalty arc ($\approx20.15$~m from the goal line). This column
was subdivided into three lateral lanes of equal width (6.11~m each).
Four depth bands were defined within this column:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{GA} (Goal Area): 0–5.5~m from the goal line ($x > 47.0$~m);
  \item \textbf{PA} (Penalty Area): 5.5–11.0~m ($x > 41.5$~m);
  \item \textbf{PE} (Penalty Edge): 11.0–16.5~m ($x > 36.0$~m);
  \item \textbf{PC} (Penalty Curve/Arc): 16.5–20.15~m ($x > 32.35$~m).
\end{itemize}
The  area between the central column and the near edge of the penalty area was called 
front zone, and the area between the central column and the sideline on the other 
side of the pitch was called edge zone. The area close to the corner taker but 
oustide of the penalty area was defined as short zone, and the rest was defined 
as 'Other'. This zone system is used for both delivery classification (SQ2) and 
player position classification (SQ1). Subsequentally, it will be used to visualise the 
typical delivery and player positioning patterns of a specific opponent (SQ5).

\section{SQ1: Defensive Corner Setup Classification}
\label{sec:sq1}

The defensive setup of a corner is operationalised as the joint configuration
of every defending outfield player at the moment of delivery. The pipeline has
two stages, motivated by the structure of the labels themselves: two of the four
defensive roles are spatially separable from position alone, while the other two
require tracking-based behavioural features to disambiguate. SQ1 therefore consists
of (i) a per-defender role classifier, (ii) a Hungarian assignment that links
each man-marker to the specific attacker they are tracking, and (iii) a per-corner
aggregation that produces the team-level defensive label used in SQ3.

\subsection{Per-Defender Role Classifier}
\label{sec:sq1_individual}

Each defending outfield player at a corner is assigned one of four roles:
\texttt{MAN} (tracks a specific attacker), \texttt{ZONAL} (covers a fixed area
of the box), \texttt{SHORT} (guards the short-corner option near the corner
flag), and \texttt{COUNTER} (positioned at the edge of the defensive half for
transition cover). Ground truth is the 46~corners in the manually labelled set
in which all ten defending outfield players were assigned a role
(see Section~\ref{sec:manual_labelling}).

\paragraph{Pair-wise tracking features.}
For every (defender, attacker) pair in a corner, six features are computed
over a pre-delivery time window:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Mean pairwise distance} between the two players;
  \item \textbf{Distance at kick} (delivery instant);
  \item \textbf{Minimum distance} attained during the window;
  \item \textbf{Distance variance} across the window;
  \item \textbf{Motion correlation}: Pearson correlation between the
        per-tick velocity components of defender and attacker, averaged over
        the $x$ and $y$ axes. High correlation indicates that the defender's
        movement tracks the attacker's, which is the kinematic signature
        of man-marking;
  \item \textbf{Tracking fraction}: the fraction of tracking frames in the
        window during which this defender was the nearest defender to that
        specific attacker.
\end{itemize}
Two windows are computed in parallel, of 3,000~ms and 5,000~ms before delivery.
The shorter window captures the immediate reactive movement before the kick;
the longer window stabilises the marking-correlation signal across the full
setup phase. Each defender's feature vector concatenates the per-pair statistics
across both windows and across all attackers, summarised as the closest-attacker
distances ($d_1$, $d_2$), best motion correlation, best tracking fraction,
and mutual-nearest-neighbour indicators (whether the defender's nearest
attacker also has the defender as their nearest defender).

\paragraph{Closest-defender ranking features.}
A tactical observation that drove an additional feature group: a defender close
to attacker $A$ may nevertheless be \emph{zonal}, not a man-marker, when another
defender is even closer to $A$. To encode this, for each defender we compute
their rank among all defenders by mean distance to their own nearest attacker,
together with the gap between rank-1 and rank-2 distances
(\texttt{rank01\_gap}).  A small gap suggests two defenders contesting the same
attacker (one is likely to be the actual marker and the other a zonal coverer);
a large gap indicates a clear, dedicated marker.

\paragraph{Two-stage classifier.}
The four classes have very different signal types. \texttt{COUNTER} defenders
are spatially well-separated (mean distance from goal $\approx 38$~m, versus
$\approx 9$~m for MAN/ZONAL); they are classified by a positional rule
(\texttt{dist\_goal\,>\,28~m}) that achieves 93\% precision and 78\% recall in
isolation. The remaining defenders are passed to a Random Forest / Extra Trees
/ Gradient Boosting ensemble, trained with class-balanced loss to handle the
SHORT/COUNTER class imbalance, that classifies them as MAN, ZONAL, or SHORT
using the pair-wise tracking features and the closest-defender ranking features
described above.

\paragraph{Validation.}
The classifier is evaluated using leave-one-corner-out cross-validation on the
46~corners with full defensive labels. Each fold trains on 45~corners and
predicts the held-out corner's ten defenders, ensuring no leakage of
match-specific patterns into the test prediction.

\subsection{Hungarian Marking Assignment}
\label{sec:sq1_marking}

For defenders classified as \texttt{MAN}, an additional step assigns each one
to the specific attacker they are tracking. Given $m$ man-markers and $n$
attackers in a corner, the cost matrix entry $C_{ij}$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
  C_{ij} = \overline{d}_{ij} - 2 \cdot \max(0, \rho_{ij}) - \tau_{ij},
  \label{eq:marking_cost}
\end{equation}
where $\overline{d}_{ij}$ is the mean pairwise distance between defender $i$ and
attacker $j$ across the 5,000~ms window, $\rho_{ij}$ is the motion correlation,
and $\tau_{ij}$ is the tracking fraction. The cost is minimised over an
$m \times n$ assignment using the Hungarian algorithm
\citep{Kuhn1955}, producing a one-to-one mapping from each predicted MAN
defender to a specific attacker. Where a defender's predicted role is incorrect,
the assignment for that defender is treated as wrong even if a more plausible
attacker exists; the marking accuracy reported in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} therefore reflects the joint performance of the role
classifier and the Hungarian step.

\subsection{Team-Level Defensive Strategy}
\label{sec:sq1_team}

Per-defender role labels are aggregated to produce a single defensive label
per corner. Following \citet{FIFA2023}, the marking intensity is defined as the
number of MAN-classified defenders in the corner, with the categorisation
\textit{predominantly zonal} ($\leq 1$ MAN), \textit{mixed} ($2$--$3$ MAN), and
\textit{predominantly man-marking} ($\geq 4$ MAN). Two binary indicators
complement this: a \textit{short defender present} flag (whether any defender
is classified as SHORT) and a \textit{counter defender present} flag (whether
any defender is classified as COUNTER). The triple
\textit{(intensity, short, counter)} is the team-level \textbf{defensive strategy
type} of the corner and serves as the defensive predictor variable in SQ3.

\subsection{Team Profile Estimation}
\label{sec:sq1_team_profile}

For each club with a minimum of ten corners as the defending team in the
dataset, the empirical distribution over defensive strategy types is computed
across all their defended corners. This distribution constitutes the team's
\textit{defensive profile} and is consumed by the corners module of the Data
\& Video Analyser (Section~\ref{sec:sq5_dv_analyser}) as the basis for
opposition scouting.

\section{SQ2: Offensive Corner Pattern Classification}
\label{sec:sq2}

Offensive corner patterns are characterised along three independent dimensions:
(i) delivery type, (ii) ball flight trajectory, and (iii) attacker movement
patterns. Together, these define the \textit{offensive corner profile} for each
corner.

\subsection{Delivery Type}
\label{sec:sq2_delivery}

Delivery type is defined as the combination of swing direction and target zone.

\subsubsection{Swing direction.}
Swing direction is determined from the kicker's foot and the corner side using
the rule: a right-footed kick from the right corner flag produces an inswing
(\texttt{IN}); the same foot from the left corner flag produces an outswing
(\texttt{OUT}). Left foot follows the mirror rule. Where foot data are ambiguous
(\texttt{FEET}), the swing label provided by the SciSports event pipeline is
used. Swing direction is validated against the manually labelled subset, yielding
92\% agreement.

\subsubsection{Target zone.}
For direct corners, the target zone is derived from the ball's endpoint
coordinates $(x_\mathrm{end}, y_\mathrm{end})$ in the event data using the zone
classification described in Section~\ref{sec:data}. Validation against the 51~manually labelled delivery zones yields the following
agreement rates: full zone label (e.g.\ \texttt{GA2}): 64\%;
depth band only (GA/PA/PE/PC): 74\%; lateral column only (near/central/far):
76\%.
The remaining discrepancies are attributable to the fact that the SciSports
event data records the ball's position at the moment of \emph{first player
contact} (header, clearance) rather than the ball's intended landing zone.
An extensive comparison of alternative tracking-based methods
(ball position at minimum forward distance, at mid-flight time, and at peak
height) found none to exceed 26\% zone accuracy, confirming that the event
endpoint is the best available automated proxy.
Consequently, the SQ3 effectiveness analysis uses the full zone label only for
the 51~manually labelled corners, and relies on the more reliable depth band
(74\% accuracy) and swing direction (92\% accuracy) dimensions for the full
1,893-corner dataset. The full zone label for unlabelled corners should be
treated as approximate and is not used in inferential models.

\label{sec:sq2_flight}

are defined. An initial five-category scheme including a separate

meaningfully distinct tactical category from the broader 3.0–8.0~m arc:

\subsection{Short Corner Sub-types}
\label{sec:sq2_short}

Short corners are classified using a detailed taxonomy derived from 21~manually
labelled short corners, which records three components: (i) the number of passes
in the sequence (counting the corner delivery itself as pass~1), (ii) the number
of distinct attacking players involved, and (iii) the outcome of the sequence.
Labels follow the format:
\[
  \texttt{SHORT\_\{n\}pass\{k\}players-\{result\}}
\]
where $n$ is the total pass count, $k$ the number of attacking players involved,
and \textit{result} $\in$ \{\texttt{cross}, \texttt{shot}, \texttt{ballloss}\}.
For example, \texttt{SHORT\_2pass3players-cross} describes a short corner in
which the delivery is followed by one additional pass involving three attackers
before a cross is delivered.

This classification is applied to all 492 short corners in the dataset by
tracing the event sequence from the corner delivery until either a cross, shot,
or defensive ball-recovery event occurs. Only genuine PASS events (not carries
or dribbles) are counted towards the pass total. An automated classifier
achieves 81\% exact-label accuracy against the 21 ground-truth labels, with
result accuracy (cross/shot/ballloss) of 86\% and player-count accuracy of 86\%.
The four remaining discrepancies involve edge cases (crosses blocked into an
out-of-play event, or sequences spanning multiple event sequence identifiers).

For the SQ3 effectiveness analysis, short corner labels are further aggregated
into nine bucketed types based on pass count ($\leq$1, 2, $\geq$3) and outcome
(cross, shot, ballloss), ensuring a minimum cell size of $n \geq 20$
per analytical group.

\subsection{Attacker Movement and Role Classification}
\label{sec:sq2_runs}

The attacking pattern of a corner is reconstructed at two complementary
granularities: the \emph{geometric running line} of each attacker (where they
end up relative to where they started) and the \emph{tactical role} of each
attacker (what tactical function their movement serves). The two are then
combined into a single per-corner tactical category.

\subsubsection{Individual running line.}
Each attacking outfield player (excluding the corner taker) is assigned a
geometric running line label from their starting zone (sampled at $t-3$~s
before delivery) and ending zone (sampled at $t+2$~s after delivery), together
with their total displacement:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{STATIC}: displacement $< 2.5$~m; player does not make a
        meaningful run;
  \item \texttt{NEAR\_POST}: ends in GA1 or PA1 (near-post column);
  \item \texttt{FAR\_POST}: ends in GA3 or PA3 (far-post column);
  \item \texttt{CENTRAL}: ends in GA2 or PA2 (central column);
  \item \texttt{CROSSING\_FN}: starts on the far side and ends on the near
        side (far-to-near crossing run);
  \item \texttt{CROSSING\_NF}: starts on the near side and ends on the far
        side (near-to-far crossing run);
  \item \texttt{EDGE}: ends in the PE or PC depth band (positioning for
        second-phase shots);
  \item \texttt{SECOND\_PHASE}: ends outside the penalty area (second-ball
        positioning).
\end{itemize}
Running-line labels are derived rule-based from zone transitions and achieve
99.5\% leave-one-corner-out agreement with manual labels — high accuracy is
expected here because the labels are themselves defined as zone transitions.

\subsubsection{Per-attacker tactical role.}
Running lines describe \emph{geometry}; they do not describe \emph{tactical
function}. Two attackers can run identical geometric lines but play opposite
roles — one designed to receive the cross, the other to pull a defender out
of the receiver's space. A second classifier therefore assigns each attacker a
tactical role:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{TARGET} — moves toward the eventual ball-arrival point and is
        the designed receiver of the cross;
  \item \texttt{DECOY} — moves away from the ball-arrival point in order to
        pull defenders out of the receiving zone;
  \item \texttt{BLOCK\_GK} — positions extremely close to the defending
        goalkeeper before the kick, screening the keeper from the cross;
  \item \texttt{BLOCK\_DEF} — positions close to a specific defender, both
        with low movement, to prevent that defender from contesting the cross;
  \item \texttt{SECOND\_BALL} — positions around the penalty-edge zone to
        anticipate clearances and rebounds;
  \item \texttt{STATIC} — does not commit to a run; remains in their initial
        position.
\end{itemize}
Ground truth is the 86~manually labelled corners (618 attacker-rows after
filtering goalkeepers and substitutes off the pitch).

\paragraph{Role-discriminating features.}
A per-attacker feature vector is computed from the tracking data sampled at
100~ms granularity over the window $[t-3000, t+1500]$~ms. The features are
designed to capture the user-defined role definitions directly:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Movement and direction}: total displacement, position at
        setup ($t-3000$~ms), at kick, and at end-of-window ($t+1000$~ms);
        cosine similarity between the attacker's movement vector
        ($\mathrm{end} - \mathrm{setup}$) and the direction from setup
        position to the ball-arrival point. High alignment indicates a run
        \emph{toward} the ball, the kinematic signature of a TARGET; negative
        alignment indicates a run \emph{away} from the ball, characteristic
        of a DECOY.
  \item \textbf{Distance to ball-arrival point}: distance to where the ball
        is first contacted, computed at the kick instant, at the end of the
        window, and the change between the two. These are the strongest
        discriminators for TARGET versus DECOY: a TARGET ends close to the
        ball-arrival point, a DECOY ends far away.
  \item \textbf{Ranking among attackers}: the rank of this attacker (and the
        gap between rank~1 and rank~2) in the per-corner ordering by distance
        to the ball-arrival point at end-of-window. The actual receiver is
        almost always rank~1; a clear gap to rank~2 is a strong TARGET signal.
  \item \textbf{Goalkeeper proximity}: mean, minimum, and at-kick distance to
        the defending goalkeeper across the pre-delivery window. Mean
        distance below approximately 3~m is the operational signature of
        BLOCK\_GK.
  \item \textbf{Defender proximity}: mean and minimum distance to the
        nearest defender over the window. Used together with low displacement
        to identify BLOCK\_DEF.
  \item \textbf{Position-based}: distance to the goal line at kick, presence
        in the penalty area at kick, and presence around the 16-metre line
        (the SECOND\_BALL anchor zone).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Goalkeeper handling.}
Goalkeepers are normally excluded from the attacking-team pool, but in
late-game pushes for an equaliser the attacking team's goalkeeper occasionally
joins the corner. The pipeline detects this case: an attacking goalkeeper is
included as an outfield attacker only when their position at the kick frame
is past the halfway line.

\paragraph{Coordinate frame.}
Player positions in the tracking data are stored in the raw match-coordinate
frame, in which the team attacks $+x$ in one half and $-x$ in the other. The
pipeline determines the attacking direction from the ball position at the
kick frame ($\mathrm{ball}_x > 0 \Rightarrow$ attacking right; otherwise mirror)
and applies it to all per-attacker features. Event data fields (\texttt{end\_x},
\texttt{end\_y}) are already provided by SciSports in attacking-right normalised
form and therefore are not mirrored a second time. Both conventions are
verified empirically against the dataset
($\mathrm{start\_x} \approx +52.5$ for both \texttt{R}- and \texttt{L}-side
corners).

\paragraph{Classifier and validation.}
A Random Forest / Extra Trees / Gradient Boosting ensemble with class-balanced
loss is trained on the 86~labelled corners with leave-one-corner-out
cross-validation. Three label schemes are evaluated and reported in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results}: the full six-class taxonomy, a five-class scheme that
collapses BLOCK\_GK and BLOCK\_DEF into a single BLOCKER class (motivated by
the small sample size of each), and a four-class scheme that additionally
collapses SECOND\_BALL into STATIC.

\subsection{Per-Corner Tactical Category}
\label{sec:sq2_taxonomy}

The per-attacker labels (running line and tactical role) are aggregated into a
single \emph{tactical category} per corner. Each corner is assigned exactly one
category in priority order: the most specific rule that fires wins, ensuring
mutually exclusive assignment. The taxonomy is grounded in coaching vocabulary
familiar to set-piece analysts: a coach reading a category name should
recognise the tactical pattern without needing to consult a definition.
Table~\ref{tab:tactical_categories} in the Appendix lists the categories.

The taxonomy serves two purposes. Within the thesis it is the offensive
predictor in SQ3: a categorical label whose effectiveness against each
defensive strategy type is the central conditional question of the
effectiveness analysis. Within the applied D\&V Analyser
(Section~\ref{sec:sq5_dv_analyser}) it is the unit of opposition profiling:
each opponent is summarised as a distribution over these 21 categories,
hyperlinked to the corresponding video clips so that a coach can verify the
pattern visually before designing a counter-routine. Both uses depend on the
same property — a category name should be tactically meaningful without
further explanation.

\subsection{Offensive Corner Profile}
\label{sec:sq2_profile}

The offensive corner profile of a single corner is therefore the tuple:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Delivery type} (Section~\ref{sec:sq2_delivery}): swing
        direction $\times$ target zone (e.g.\ \texttt{IN\_PA2});
  \item \textbf{Ball flight} (Section~\ref{sec:sq2_flight}): one of GROUND /
        LOW / MEDIUM / FLOATED;
  \item \textbf{Tactical category} (Section~\ref{sec:sq2_taxonomy}): one of
        21 named categories from Table~\ref{tab:tactical_categories};
  \item \textbf{Short vs.\ direct} indicator;
  \item \textbf{Short corner sub-type} (Section~\ref{sec:sq2_short}): for
        short corners only.
\end{enumerate}
Direct-corner SQ3 analyses use these dimensions either marginally (a single
dimension at a time) or as the cross-product (tactical category $\times$
defensive strategy) for the central conditional comparison.

\section{SQ3: Effectiveness of Offensive Strategies Against Defensive Structures}
\label{sec:sq3}

\subsection{Outcome Variable}
\label{sec:sq3_outcome}

Following the recommendation in recent systematic reviews
\citep[e.g.][]{Plakias2025, Casal2015}, corner kick effectiveness is
measured using two complementary outcome indicators.

\subsubsection{Corner Threat Score (CTS).}
\citet{Lamberts2023} proposes the Corner Threat Score, defined as the harmonic
mean of the delivery quality (xA) and conversion quality (xG) components:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CTS} = \frac{2 \cdot \overline{\mathrm{xG}} \cdot \overline{\mathrm{xA}}}
                       {\overline{\mathrm{xG}} + \overline{\mathrm{xA}}},
  \label{eq:cts}
\end{equation}
where $\overline{\mathrm{xA}}$ is the SciSports expected-assist value on the
corner delivery event, and $\overline{\mathrm{xG}}$ is the maximum expected-goals
value of any shot attempt in the same possession sequence after the corner.
When no shot occurs in the sequence, $\overline{\mathrm{xG}} = 0$ and CTS is
set to zero, which is conservative but avoids imputation.
CTS is the \textit{primary continuous effectiveness measure}.

In this study, $\overline{\mathrm{xG}}$ is extracted by tracing each corner's
\texttt{sequenceId} through the raw match event files and selecting the highest
xG value among all subsequent \texttt{SHOT}, \texttt{HEADER}, and
\texttt{SHOT\_FREE\_KICK} events in that sequence.

A property of CTS that warrants explicit acknowledgement is its behaviour
under sequence-level zero-inflation. When the corner does not generate a
shot, $\overline{\mathrm{xG}} = 0$ and CTS collapses to zero regardless of
delivery quality. As a consequence, CTS aggregated over a group of corners
mixes two underlying quantities: the probability that the corner produces
any shot and the conditional quality of that shot. This is conceptually
desirable for an effectiveness measure but means that the harmonic-mean
form is, in practice, dominated by shot-generation rate when the rate is
low. The complementary shot-generation indicator is therefore retained as a
second outcome to allow these two components to be examined separately
(Section~\ref{sec:sq3_analysis}).

\subsubsection{Shot generation.}
A binary indicator ($y_1 \in \{0,1\}$) of whether any shot event occurs within
the corner sequence. Shot generation reflects whether the corner created
a scoring opportunity independent of shot quality, and is used alongside CTS
in the regression analysis.

\subsection{Analytical Strategy}
\label{sec:sq3_analysis}

The effectiveness analysis proceeds in three steps.

\subsubsection{Step 1 — Descriptive cross-tabulation.}
For each combination of offensive dimension and defensive strategy type,
mean CTS, mean xA, mean sequence xG, and shot rate are computed and displayed
as a two-dimensional effectiveness matrix. Fisher's exact test is applied to shot
generation counts for each cell pair; the Bonferroni correction adjusts for
multiple comparisons. Only cells with $n \geq 15$ corners are reported; those
with $n < 15$ are noted as insufficiently powered.

\subsubsection{Step 2 — Logistic regression (shot generation).}
A logistic regression model is fitted with shot generation ($y_1$) as the
outcome:
\begin{equation}
  \log\frac{P(y_1=1)}{P(y_1=0)} = \beta_0 +
    \beta_1 \cdot \mathrm{swing} +
    \beta_2 \cdot \mathrm{zone\_depth} +
    \beta_3 \cdot \mathrm{flight} +
    \beta_4 \cdot \mathrm{setup\_type} +
    \beta_5 \cdot \mathrm{def\_strategy} +
    \beta_6 \cdot (\mathrm{swing} \times \mathrm{def\_strategy}) + \varepsilon,
  \label{eq:logit}
\end{equation}
where categorical predictors are dummy-coded. The interaction term tests whether
the effectiveness of a particular swing direction differs by defensive strategy
type — the core hypothesis of SQ3. Model fit is assessed using the area under
the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC-ROC) and the log-likelihood
ratio test against an intercept-only baseline.

\subsubsection{Step 3 — Linear regression (CTS).}
An ordinary least squares (OLS) regression of the same predictors on CTS is
estimated alongside Equation~\ref{eq:logit}. Because CTS is heavily
zero-inflated by construction, a two-part hurdle model
\citep[see][for the approach in sports contexts]{Rathke2017}
is estimated as a robustness check: a logistic component models whether
CTS~$> 0$ (equivalent to shot generation), and a linear component models
the magnitude of CTS conditional on a shot occurring. Substantive
conclusions are drawn only where the OLS and hurdle estimates agree.

\subsubsection{Sample Size and Power}
\label{sec:sq3_power}

With 1,401 direct corners, a swing dimension (2 levels) and three defensive
strategy types (3 levels), the average cell size is approximately
$1401 / (2 \times 3) \approx 233$ corners. Interaction cells using the full
delivery type code (22 levels) are considerably smaller; these are therefore
not included in the regression model, and the analysis restricts the delivery
dimension to the coarser decomposition in Table~\ref{tab:offensive_dims}.

\section{SQ4: Individual Player Qualities and Corner Success}
\label{sec:sq4}

\subsection{Corner Taker Quality}
\label{sec:sq4_taker}

The quality of the corner taker is characterised using three metrics.

\subsubsection{Delivery accuracy.}
For the 51~corners in the manually labelled set where the intended delivery zone
is known, delivery accuracy is defined as the proportion of corners landing in
the intended depth band (GA vs.\ PA vs.\ PE or deeper). This is reported as a
taker-specific statistic for takers with $\geq 5$~labelled deliveries.

\subsubsection{Delivery quality score (CTS-based).}
Taker quality is quantified as the \textit{Corner Threat Score} computed at the
taker level \citep{Lamberts2023}: the CTS formula (Equation~\ref{eq:cts}) is
applied to the taker's mean xA and mean sequence xG aggregated across all their
corners in the dataset:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{TakerCTS} = \frac{2 \cdot \overline{\mathrm{xG}}_{\mathrm{taker}}
                              \cdot \overline{\mathrm{xA}}_{\mathrm{taker}}}
                           {\overline{\mathrm{xG}}_{\mathrm{taker}}
                              + \overline{\mathrm{xA}}_{\mathrm{taker}}},
  \label{eq:taker_cts}
\end{equation}
where $\overline{\mathrm{xA}}_{\mathrm{taker}}$ is the taker's mean SciSports
expected assist per delivery and $\overline{\mathrm{xG}}_{\mathrm{taker}}$ is
the mean maximum shot xG in their corner sequences. TakerCTS integrates both
delivery quality and the threat generated by the taker's corners.
Only takers with $\geq 10$~corners in the dataset are assigned a score.

\subsubsection{Delivery consistency.}
The standard deviation of ball peak height ($\sigma_{z}$) across a taker's corners
is computed as a measure of trajectory consistency. Lower $\sigma_{z}$ indicates
greater repeatability of the delivery arc. Flight category entropy is reported
as a complementary measure.

\subsection{Target Player Quality}
\label{sec:sq4_target}

Where a shot or header immediately follows the corner delivery and the initiating
player can be identified from event data, that player is defined as the
\textit{aerial target}. Two quality indicators are extracted:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Aerial duel win rate}: the proportion of aerial duels won by the
        player across all match events (not limited to corners), computed from the
        full event dataset;
  \item \textbf{Physical attributes}: player height (metres), if available in the
        SciSports player metadata. Where not available, this metric is omitted and
        flagged as a data gap.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Controlling for Player Quality in SQ3}
\label{sec:sq4_control}

To isolate the effect of tactical setup (delivery type, defensive strategy) from
individual player contributions, the logistic regression in
Equation~\ref{eq:logit} is re-estimated after adding taker CTS and (where
available) target player height as additional covariates. A decrease in the
coefficients of tactical predictors after inclusion of player quality variables
would indicate that part of the observed tactical effectiveness is attributable
to individual quality rather than the tactic itself. This analysis addresses the
primary research question of SQ4.

\section{SQ5: Tactical Preparation Tool}
\label{sec:sq5}

\subsection{Overview}
\label{sec:sq5_overview}

SQ5 translates the analytical outputs of SQ1–SQ4 into an applied preparation
artefact intended for use within FC Den Bosch's analysis workflow. The
deliverable consists of two components: (A) integration of the corner-kick
findings into the club's in-development \textit{Data \& Video Analyser}
(D\&V Analyser), and (B) a positional response model, referred to
informally as the \textit{magnet board}, which sits inside Component A as
its corners-specific interactive view.

\subsection{Component A: Integration into the Data \& Video Analyser}
\label{sec:sq5_dv_analyser}

\subsubsection{Scope and rationale.}
In parallel to the present thesis, the author is developing a Data \& Video
Analyser to be deployed at FC Den Bosch from the 2026–2027 season onward.
The D\&V Analyser is a unified pre- and post-match preparation tool that
aligns event-level analytics (build-up, key passes, crosses, shots,
defensive transitions, set pieces) with the corresponding match video
clips, allowing coaching staff to move directly from a tabulated event or
metric to the underlying footage. Whereas existing third-party platforms
typically present numbers and video as separate artefacts, the D\&V
Analyser is designed around a single principle: every quantitative claim
shown to a coach is one click away from the video evidence that produced
it.
The corners module of the D\&V Analyser is populated by the outputs of
this thesis. The integration described below therefore constitutes the
applied contribution of the work and is the channel through which the
findings reach the practitioners they are intended for.

\subsubsection{Data flow into the corners module.}
For each corner kick in the historical dataset, the pipeline produced in
SQ1–SQ4 yields a structured record containing: (i) the offensive corner
profile (delivery type, ball-flight category, attacker movement pattern,
short-corner sub-type where applicable), (ii) the defensive setup of the
opposing team (counts of zonal, man-marking, short, and counter-press
roles, plus the derived defensive strategy label), (iii) the corner's
effectiveness measures (CTS, sequence xG, shot generation, goal), and
(iv) the corner taker's identity and TakerCTS. Each record is keyed by
\texttt{(match\_id, start\_time\_ms)}, which doubles as the join key to
the D\&V Analyser's video index: the millisecond timestamp aligned to
match clock identifies the exact frame at which the corner is taken,
enabling the D\&V Analyser to retrieve and play the corresponding clip on
demand. The corners module therefore consumes the
\texttt{corner\_cts.csv}, \texttt{attacking\_corner\_profiles.csv}, and
the per-team aggregates produced in SQ1–SQ4 as static inputs, joined to
the live video index at query time.

\subsubsection{Opponent profile view.}
For a given upcoming opponent, the corners module presents a tabulated
profile assembled from the data flow described above. On the offensive
side, this profile reports the opponent's empirical distribution over
delivery types, ball-flight categories, dominant running-line patterns,
and short-corner sub-types from their corners as the attacking team, with
the most-frequent combinations surfaced as the opponent's modal
attacking patterns. On the defensive side, it reports the empirical
distribution over defensive role counts (e.g.\ ``four zonal, two
man-markers, one short'') and the derived defensive strategy labels from
SQ1, observed across that opponent's corners as the defending team. Each
row in the profile is hyperlinked to the underlying corners; clicking a
delivery-type row, for example, plays the video clips of every corner the
opponent has taken into that delivery type, allowing the analyst to
verify whether the empirical distribution reflects a genuine tactical
preference or a small-sample artefact.

\subsubsection{Effectiveness recommendation view.}
Adjacent to the opponent profile, the corners module presents the
output of the SQ3 effectiveness analysis conditioned on the opponent's
modal defensive setup: which offensive dimensions have generated the
highest CTS, given that defensive context, across the league. This is
not used as a deterministic recommendation; rather, it is presented as a
ranked list of \textit{candidate} attacking approaches, with both the
point estimate and an indication of supporting sample size, so that
coaches can weigh tactical advice against statistical confidence. Where
sample sizes are too small to support comparison (typically the
finer-grained delivery $\times$ defence cells), the cell is flagged
rather than ranked. This treatment follows directly from the
methodological caution discussed in Section~\ref{sec:sq3_power}.

\subsubsection{Video-anchored validation loop.}
The presence of the video link is methodologically important rather than
merely cosmetic. A persistent risk in tactical analytics is that
automated labels (defensive strategy types, running lines, delivery
zones) drift from the tactical reality they purport to describe. By
making every aggregate one click away from the underlying clips, the D\&V
Analyser embeds a continuous validation loop: when an aggregate appears
to disagree with the coach's tactical reading, the clips that produced
the aggregate are immediately inspectable. This is the practical channel
through which the limitations identified in Sections~\ref{sec:sq2_delivery}
and~\ref{sec:sq2_runs} (e.g.\ delivery zones inferred from event endpoint
rather than intended target, large fraction of corners without complete
tracking) are surfaced to the user rather than hidden inside aggregate
numbers.

\subsubsection{Position within the thesis.}
The D\&V Analyser itself is engineering work and is not in scope for the
thesis. What is in scope is (i) the design specification of the corners
module and its data contract with the underlying analyses
(this section), (ii) the magnet-board view embedded within it
(Section~\ref{sec:sq5_magnet}), and (iii) a worked example for one
opponent demonstrating the end-to-end flow from raw corners to surfaced
recommendation, which is reported in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}. The
purpose of this component is to make the analytical contribution of
SQ1–SQ4 actionable in a setting that practitioners actually use, and to
ground the thesis's claim of practical relevance in a concrete artefact
rather than an abstract recommendation.

\subsection{Component B: Positional Response Model (Magnet Board)}
\label{sec:sq5_magnet}

The magnet board addresses the question: given a planned attacker configuration,
what defensive positioning is the opposing team likely to adopt, and where do
structural weaknesses appear?

\subsubsection{Representation.}
Each corner is represented by two spatial configurations, extracted from tracking
data at the delivery moment ($t = 0$):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Attacker configuration}: the $(x, y)$ coordinates of all
        attacking outfield players (excluding the corner taker), recorded at
        $t - 3$~s (pre-delivery setup);
  \item \textbf{Defender configuration}: the $(x, y)$ coordinates of all
        defending outfield players at $t = 0$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Zonal defender positions.}
For teams with $\geq 10$~historical corners as the defending team, the mean
position of each defender classified as \texttt{ZONAL} is computed across all
their corners. This yields a \textit{zonal template}: a set of mean positions
for each zonal slot (typically 4–6 players), visualised as labelled markers
on a 2D pitch diagram. The zonal template is relatively stable across corners
for a given team, reflecting deliberate set-piece preparation; the standard
deviation of each position is reported to quantify consistency.

\subsubsection{Man-marker response.}
For defenders classified as \texttt{MAN}, the model estimates where a specific
man-marker is likely to position given an attacker placed at a given location.
Because man-markers track their assigned attacker, their expected position at
delivery is approximated by the attacker's delivery-moment position plus a
small offset vector derived from the mean positional lag observed in the
historical data. Formally, let $\mathbf{a}$ denote the planned attacker position
and $\hat{\boldsymbol{\delta}}$ denote the mean tracking offset:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{d}} = \mathbf{a} + \hat{\boldsymbol{\delta}},
  \label{eq:man_marker}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\boldsymbol{\delta}}$ is estimated from the 95~man-marking pairs in
the manually labelled dataset. This offset captures the fact that man-markers
typically position slightly between their assigned attacker and the goal.

\subsubsection{Gap identification.}
Given a planned attacker configuration and the predicted defensive response
(zonal positions + man-marker responses), the uncovered pitch area is estimated
by computing, for each of the 12 central zones (GA1–PC3), the minimum distance
from any defensive player to the zone centroid. Zones with minimum distance
exceeding a threshold (calibrated on the labelled dataset) are flagged as
\textit{potentially open}, providing actionable guidance on which zone to target
with the delivery.

\subsubsection{Visualisation and interpretation.}
The magnet board is rendered as a standard 2D pitch diagram
(attacking half only) showing:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Zonal defenders as circles labelled with their role, at their expected
        mean position ($\pm$ one standard deviation ellipse);
  \item Man-markers as arrows from the planned attacker position to the
        predicted defender position;
  \item Potentially open zones highlighted in the target-zone grid.
\end{itemize}
The output is designed to be immediately legible to coaching staff without
statistical expertise. No machine learning model is used in the magnet board;
the tool is entirely rule-based and transparent.

\subsubsection{Limitation.}
The magnet board is trained on aggregate historical data and cannot account for
within-team variation or tactical adjustments in response to specific attacker
identities that the opposing coach has pre-planned. It should be used as a
probabilistic starting point for tactical discussion rather than a deterministic
prediction.
